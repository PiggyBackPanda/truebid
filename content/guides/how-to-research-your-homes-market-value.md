Pricing your home is the part that makes most private sellers nervous. Underprice and you leave money on the table. Overprice and your listing sits there growing stale while buyers scroll past. Both outcomes are real risks. The good news is that the data you need to price well is mostly free and publicly available -- you just need to know where to look and what to do with it.

## Listed price vs. sold price

This is the most important distinction in property research: the price a seller asks for is not the price buyers actually pay.

Listing prices are aspirational. They reflect what the seller hopes to get, often influenced by what they paid, what a neighbour got, or what an agent suggested to win the listing. Sold prices are facts. They reflect what a buyer agreed to hand over, after inspecting the property, comparing alternatives, and deciding this was the best option at that price.

Always research sold prices, not listing prices.

## Where to find sold data

**Domain and realestate.com.au** both have sold property sections. Filter by suburb and date range. You can see sale prices for most properties sold in the past few years, along with photos and listing descriptions. This is your starting point.

**CoreLogic** is the data provider behind most of the industry's automated valuations. They offer paid property reports (usually $30 to $50) that include recent comparable sales, suburb statistics, and an estimated value range. If you want to go deeper, it's worth the cost.

**Your state's land titles office** publishes official sales data. In Western Australia this is Landgate, in Victoria it's Land Use Victoria, and so on. The data is authoritative but less user-friendly than Domain or CoreLogic.

**RP Data** and **PriceFinder** are professional tools used by agents. Some accountants or buyers agents will run a report for you for a fee if you ask.

## Choosing comparable sales

Not every sold property in your suburb is a useful comparison. You want sales that closely match your property on the factors that actually drive value.

A useful comparable is:
- In your suburb or the immediately adjacent one
- Similar in size: within 15 to 20 per cent of your floor area
- On a similar land size (for houses) or same complex (for units)
- In similar condition -- if yours is renovated, compare to renovated properties
- Sold within the past six months

The tighter your comparables, the more reliable your estimate. If you can find five or six genuinely similar sales in the last three months, you're working with good data. If you're stretching to find anything comparable, you need to be more cautious.

## Adjusting for differences

No two properties are identical, so you need to adjust your estimates up or down based on how your property differs from your comparables.

A pool can add $20,000 to $60,000 depending on the market. A main road or power line can subtract a similar amount. A recently renovated kitchen is worth something. A dated bathroom or no parking reduces value. The view from the backyard matters.

The adjustments are somewhat subjective, but you can calibrate them by looking at pairs of sold properties that differ on just one factor. Find two similar houses in the same street -- one with a pool, one without -- and you get a reasonable sense of what the pool is worth in that market.

## The anchoring problem

Two numbers will try to influence your pricing and you should actively resist both of them.

The first is what you paid. What you paid is irrelevant to what the market will pay today. Markets move. Your purchase price is historical data, not a floor.

The second is what a neighbour got. One sale is not a trend. A neighbour's result might reflect a motivated buyer, a clever agent, or an unusual circumstance. Use it as a single data point, not as your benchmark.

Both of these anchors are emotionally compelling and factually unreliable. Notice when you're using them and go back to the comparable sales data.

## Get some free appraisals

Even if you have no intention of using an agent, getting two or three independent appraisals from local agents is a sensible step. It is free. Agents do this hoping to win the listing, and there is nothing dishonest about taking their opinion without signing anything.

Be transparent -- tell them you are researching your options and not necessarily going to list through an agent. Most will still come and give you a figure. You might get inflated numbers from agents trying to flatter you into signing up, which is why you want at least two or three opinions you can compare against each other and against your own research.

Treat the appraisals as reference points, not verdicts. If three agents all land between $750,000 and $800,000 and your own research suggests $780,000, you're working from a solid base.

## How Open Offers helps with pricing uncertainty

One advantage of selling through Open Offers is that you set a floor price -- the minimum you will accept -- and the market tells you the real value through competition.

If buyers are interested, they will place an offer. If you set the floor too low, the competition will push the final price up. If you set it right, you capture the genuine market value without having to guess it perfectly in advance. The format reduces the cost of being slightly wrong on price because the process self-corrects.

This does not mean you should set an arbitrary floor. You still need to do the research so you know what a fair floor looks like. But it does mean pricing anxiety matters a bit less with this format than with a fixed-price sale where the number you pick is the number you get.

## Pricing is mostly science

The property industry sometimes makes pricing sound like a mysterious art that only agents can practise. It is not. It is arithmetic applied to data, with some judgement around differences. The data is publicly available. The method is straightforward. What takes practice is doing it without letting emotion get in the way.

Set aside what you paid. Set aside what you hoped for. Look at what buyers actually paid for genuinely comparable properties in the past few months, adjust for the differences between those properties and yours, triangulate with a couple of agent appraisals, and you will have a number you can defend. That is all a professional valuation does, and you can do it yourself.
