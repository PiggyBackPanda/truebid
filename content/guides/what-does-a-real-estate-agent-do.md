Selling without an agent raises an obvious question: what exactly am I giving up?

It's a fair thing to ask. Agents do provide real value for some sellers in some situations. The goal here isn't to talk you out of using one — it's to lay out what they actually do so you can make an informed call about whether the commission is worth it for you.

## What an agent typically does

### Appraisal and pricing advice

Before listing, an agent will walk through your property and give you a market appraisal — an estimate of what it should sell for based on recent comparable sales in your area.

This is genuinely useful if you have no idea where to start. That said, appraisals are estimates, not guarantees, and agents have an incentive to pitch a number that wins your business. Independent research using recent sales data (which is publicly available) gets you most of the way there. There's a full guide on pricing your property in this series.

### Photography and marketing setup

Most agents coordinate professional photography, write the listing copy, and prepare the floor plan. They'll have relationships with photographers they use regularly.

You can organise all of this yourself. A good real estate photographer costs $200 to $500 depending on your market. Writing clear, accurate copy isn't hard — you know your home better than the agent does.

### Listing on the major portals

In most Australian states, private sellers can list directly on realestate.com.au and Domain through third-party listing services. TrueBid handles your listing on our platform and can point you toward portal access options.

This used to be a genuine barrier to selling privately. It largely isn't anymore.

### Running open homes and private inspections

Agents manage the scheduling, show buyers through, and collect contact details. This takes time and requires you to be available (or to trust someone else in your home).

If you sell privately, you do this yourself. That means weekend availability during your campaign and being prepared to answer questions on the spot. Some sellers find this fine. Others find it stressful. Be honest with yourself about which camp you're in.

### Handling buyer enquiries

Agents field calls and emails from interested buyers, answer questions, and filter out tyre-kickers before they get to you.

You'll handle this directly when selling privately. It's manageable with a dedicated email and a clear listing that answers common questions upfront.

### Negotiating offers

This is where experienced agents arguably earn the most of their commission. A good agent knows how to hold firm, create urgency, and work multiple interested buyers against each other to push the price up.

That said, in a transparent offer environment like TrueBid's Open Offers system, a lot of the negotiation dynamic is built into the process itself. Buyers can see competing offers in real time, which creates genuine competitive pressure without requiring anyone to manage it manually. The anti-snipe extension prevents last-second lowball wins.

Private treaty negotiations — where you're going back and forth in private — are harder to manage alone and closer to where agent experience matters most.

### Coordinating with conveyancers through to settlement

Once an offer is accepted, there's a period of paperwork, conditions, and coordination with conveyancers on both sides. Agents typically act as the liaison during this phase.

Your conveyancer handles almost all of this. Their job is to manage the legal transfer, draft the contract, and make sure settlement goes through correctly. You're the point of contact between your conveyancer and the buyer — which mostly means passing messages and responding promptly.

## What's honest to acknowledge

Experienced agents in active markets do sometimes achieve better prices than sellers would on their own. They know the local buyers, they're skilled at working multiple parties, and they're not emotionally attached to the property the way you are.

If your property is unusual, in a slow market, or needs careful positioning, the case for an agent is stronger.

If your property is in a competitive suburb, priced fairly, and you're willing to do the work — the case is weaker.

## What's left for you to manage

When you strip it back, selling privately means handling photography, writing your listing, running open homes, fielding enquiries, and communicating with your conveyancer.
